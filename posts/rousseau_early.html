March 10, 2025

Notes to the introduction

Rousseau lived from June 28, 1712 to July 2, 1778.

The first discourse–Discourse on the Sciences and the Arts (1750)

“two poles” for ways of life:

  1. Cato the Younger: vita activa, public life
  2. Socrates: vita contemplativa, private life, also life of the “pre-political savage”

Politics as “the organizing perspective.” (xii) Rousseau regarded “political life as our medium” (ibid.)

Rousseau was a close reader of Plutarch (author of Parallel Lives).

In his negative response to the Dijon Academy’s Question on the effect of culture on morality,

“[Rousseau’s] argument is not that all uncultured, savage, or barbarous nations are necessarily morally/politically excellent, but that assigning priority to ‘culture’ in the public life threatens and, in the long run, destroys freedom and justice.” (xiv)

“Moral/political excellence … can only be achieved by everyone’s recognizing the shared concern for the common interest or good as the organizing principle of their cares and pursuits, in short by the education–or re-education–of the passions.” (xv)

“it follows that anything that causes the citizens to be distracted from pursuing and preserving political freedom threatens it. That is why the pursuit of the arts and sciences to the neglect of civic virtue imperils political freedom. That is also why Rousseau rejects the argument that the unfettered pursuit of private interest redounds to the public interest” (xvi)
